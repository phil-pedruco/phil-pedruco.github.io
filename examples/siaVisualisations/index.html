Modelling Integrated Urban Water Management

I created a series of d3.js visualisation for a colleague of mine back in May 2013 the aim of which was to demonstrate interactions between different different aspects of urban water management.

In the end we decided to use Chord Diagrams to highlight linkages between Trade-offs and Interactions for Objectives and Solutions. In these diagrams the objectives are the aims of particular projects, studies or policies and solutions are the interventions that can be used to achieve these outcomes.

Another aspect that we wanted to visualise was the complex path we needed to get from our objectives to the solutions. We also wanted to show how our solutions related to the the measurement criteria as well as the tools we use to design these. To achieve this we used the Parallel Sets d3.js plugin designed by Jason Davies.

As this post is about the visualisation there's no discussion integrated water cycle management, just about the visulaisation. However, if you're interested in managing water in the urban environment Melbourne Water are a good source of information.

Chord Diagrams

The purpose of the Chord Diagrams was to highlight the inter-connectivity between all the different aspects of the urban water management. If you click figure below you'll be taken to page with that explores the Trade-offs and Interaction between Objectives. You can see that the Objectives are visually encoded by colour with the traditional hard engineering objectives shown in a brown colour, the tangible green objectives are shown in the cream colour and intangible green objectives are shown in an orange colour.

The links between objectives are coloured to reflect the more (generally) prominent objective group. This can be highlighted by hovering over the name or the bar around the circumference of the diagram. This interaction exposes some interesting results and perhaps some, sadly, obvious ones. For instance our transitional hard engineering solutions are dominant. On the other hand Aesthetics dominate Waterway Health.

Parallel Sets

A Parallel Sets visualisation was used explore the connections between how we measure our criteria to see if our solutions are actually working and what tools we use to do that. Before I go too much further I really need to acknowledge how good this plugin is, it's just fantastic. There is tonnes of interaction and the ability to reorder data and see things in a different way.

This is a complex visualisation that allows us to explore the data is many different ways. Initially you can see how solutions are mapped to our criteria and what tools we use to measure these criteria. Again things are colour encoded with greener engineering options being ... green. Harder engineering options are browns with management options yellow. The size of the path represents the relative number of links. Hover over the title it will highlight the paths to solutions and it also displays some information about the category including a count of the sub-categories. You also get the option to reorder the categories alphabetically or by size and hovering over any path and it will highlight all the connections.

If you want to view the data in a different order, say with the Criteria at the top, all you have to do is grab the heading Criteria and drag it to the top. This allows us to transform the data and look at it in a completely different was.
